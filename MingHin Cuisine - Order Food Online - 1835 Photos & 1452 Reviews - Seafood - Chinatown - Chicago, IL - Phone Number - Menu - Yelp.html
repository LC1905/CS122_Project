      Fabulous relatively high-end Chinese food joint. This is a local chain with three (?) locations. The menu has photos for most items so laowai can order with confidence. Service actually exists and is prompt, which is a healthy departure from most authentic restaurants where the service staff treats you like a nuisance. The food is very, very good and expertly prepared. It's probably some of the best you can get in Chicago and outside of "first tier" North American Chinese food cities like NYC, LA, SF, Toronto, and Vancouver.

      We really enjoyed the Loop location of Ming Hin recently, so decided to check out the Chinatown location after visiting the Terra Cotta Warriors at the Field. If you're heading over, know that the El Chinatown stop is a block away, or there's a public lot ($2 with validation). In summer, you can also take the water taxi. This location is a lot bigger than it might appear - there are 2 big dining rooms upstairs and plenty of space for big groups. Unlike traditional dim sum places, there's no cart here - you mark your stuff on a sheet and they bring it out, hot and fresh. We tried 5-6 things: siu mai (freshly made), pork crepes, country dumplings, veggie steam buns, potstickers and pork fried rice. I might be forgetting something? Anyway, everything we had was really good! I especially liked the stream buns and siu mai. Prices are crazy cheap - all of the above plus tea and a beer? $34! Portions are generous and the quality is high. Staff was super nice and lightning fast as well. There are lots of options in Chinatown but there's a reason that MingHin is rated so well - it's tasty, affordable and friendly.

      MingHin = some of the best dim sum I've had. Anywhere. This Chinese restaurant is located in Chicago's Chinatown Square and it's actually nicer than most dim sum restaurants I've eaten at. They've fancied this place up and made it look clean and classy. It's not just another run-of-the-mill or hole-in-the-wall Cantonese joint. It's got some elegance to it. At dim sum, they don't use pushcarts. Instead, dim sum is ordered off the menu. I was a little apprehensive about this at first, but all of the dishes came out lightning quick and they were hot and delicious. Dim sum is served daily from 8am to 4pm. They have over 60 authentic items and they are priced as follows: * Small - $3.25 * Medium - $3.95 * Large - $4.50 Special dishes run upwards of $5. All pretty affordable. Also available during dim sum hours are cold appetizers, Cantonese BBQ, and noodle and fried rice dishes ($5-7 each). Our group of four showed up around 11:20am on a Tuesday morning and were seated immediately. By the time we left, the place was packed, so it appears to be quite popular. Here's what we went with: * Chilled jelly fish * Chicken feet * BBQ pork bun (a.k.a. char siu bao) * Siu mai - pork & shrimp dumplings * BBQ pork crepe (a.k.a. chee cheong fun, rice noodle rolls) * Shrimp crepe (a.k.a. chee cheong fun, rice noodle rolls) * Beef tripe with ginger and onion * Lotus seed paste bun * Fried rice noodle beef with soy sauce (a.k.a. beef chow fun) Everything was great. There wasn't a single item that I didn't like. I think my friends agreed. The only thing that tasted a bit off was the jellyfish, but I still enjoyed it. Cold jellyfish is a staple Cantonese dish that I ate a lot growing up because my sister always ordered it. My personal favorite growing up was chicken feet and I really liked it here. Bone-in, skin-on, fatty, tendony, and flavorful. Mmm... My parents always loved to order the char siu bao (steamed BBQ pork buns). The white buns were fluffy, soft and filled with hot, savory BBQ pork. Char siu bao is filling and great for the road since it packs well and isn't messy to eat. Recommended. The siu mai were meaty and tasty. The rice noodle rolls were on point (meaty as well). We liked the tripe so much that we got three orders of it. Its texture is not for everyone, but we are big fans. Excellent sauce/flavor. The lotus seed paste bun is a sweet paste, so this bun could be considered somewhat of a breakfast item or a dessert. Good stuff. And you can never go wrong with beef chow fun. Stir-fried rice noodles, beef, bean sprouts, scallions -- Cantonese comfort food at its finest and another one of my favorites. If you like spicy food, try it with chili sauce. Service was terrific. I would love to come back.

      This Cantonese restaurant has been my go-to spot whenever I am craving for dim sum in the area. During my last visit, the spacious interior was already crowded with families and groups seated at every turn. Fortunately, we didn't have to wait very long for a booth. The hot jasmine tea arrived quickly, and we ordered from a paper menu by checking off a number of photos of their dim sum items. These dishes were delivered straight from the kitchen. There were no carts in sight to mediate the traditional yum cha experience. The Shrimp Dumplings ($3.85) came with a decent amount of shrimp with an exterior that could be more refined. It was decent, but not outstanding. The Bean Curd Skin with Pork and Shrimp ($3.85) came with slippery dried bean curd skin. The filling was a bit off balanced with more pork than shrimp. The Curry Baby Cuttlefish ($4.25) were very tender and nicely steeped in a yellow Chinese curry. I wished the curry flavor could be more pointed, but it was our favorite item due to its creativity and taste. The Pan-Fried Turnip Cake ($3.85) was made with grated turnip with touches of Chinese BBQ pork here and there. The turnip was tender, but I wished it came with crispy edges to add dimensions to this dish. It was decent, but not outstanding. The Crab and Chicken Congee ($8.95) came with a large bowl of boiling crab and chicken congee. There were plenty of crab and chicken, but the crab was not de-shelled, and made it very difficult to consume. The consistency of the congee was decent, but not as thick and creamy as I would have liked. Not recommended. Service was prompt and efficient. The experience reminded me of Jing Fong, or many of the fast food dim sum spots on the streets of San Francisco. Despite the strengths and shortcomings described, I believe this can be a good place for groups, for people who don't have regular access to authentic dim sum restaurants, and for late night dining. I hope to find better dim sum restaurants in the area before coming back.

        MingHin = some of the best dim sum I've had. Anywhere.

        This Chinese restaurant is located in Chicago's Chinatown Square and it's actually nicer than most dim sum restaurants I've eaten at. They've fancied this place up and made it look clean and classy. It's not just another run-of-the-mill or hole-in-the-wall Cantonese joint. It's got some elegance to it. At dim sum, they don't use pushcarts. Instead, dim sum is ordered off the menu. I was a little apprehensive about this at first, but all of the dishes came out lightning quick and they were hot and delicious.

        Dim sum is served daily from 8am to 4pm. They have over 60 authentic items and they are priced as follows:

        * Small - $3.25
        * Medium - $3.95
        * Large - $4.50

        Special dishes run upwards of $5. All pretty affordable. Also available during dim sum hours are cold appetizers, Cantonese BBQ, and noodle and fried rice dishes ($5-7 each).

        Our group of four showed up around 11:20am on a Tuesday morning and were seated immediately. By the time we left, the place was packed, so it appears to be quite popular.

        Here's what we went with:

        * Chilled jelly fish
        * Chicken feet
        * BBQ pork bun (a.k.a. char siu bao)
        * Siu mai - pork & shrimp dumplings
        * BBQ pork crepe (a.k.a. chee cheong fun, rice noodle rolls)
        * Shrimp crepe (a.k.a. chee cheong fun, rice noodle rolls)
        * Beef tripe with ginger and onion
        * Lotus seed paste bun
        * Fried rice noodle beef with soy sauce (a.k.a. beef chow fun)

        Everything was great. There wasn't a single item that I didn't like. I think my friends agreed. The only thing that tasted a bit off was the jellyfish, but I still enjoyed it. Cold jellyfish is a staple Cantonese dish that I ate a lot growing up because my sister always ordered it. My personal favorite growing up was chicken feet and I really liked it here. Bone-in, skin-on, fatty, tendony, and flavorful. Mmm...

        My parents always loved to order the char siu bao (steamed BBQ pork buns). The white buns were fluffy, soft and filled with hot, savory BBQ pork. Char siu bao is filling and great for the road since it packs well and isn't messy to eat. Recommended.

        The siu mai were meaty and tasty. The rice noodle rolls were on point (meaty as well). We liked the tripe so much that we got three orders of it. Its texture is not for everyone, but we are big fans. Excellent sauce/flavor.

        The lotus seed paste bun is a sweet paste, so this bun could be considered somewhat of a breakfast item or a dessert. Good stuff.

        And you can never go wrong with beef chow fun. Stir-fried rice noodles, beef, bean sprouts, scallions -- Cantonese comfort food at its finest and another one of my favorites. If you like spicy food, try it with chili sauce.

        Service was terrific. I would love to come back.

        This Cantonese restaurant has been my go-to spot whenever I am craving for dim sum in the area. During my last visit, the spacious interior was already crowded with families and groups seated at every turn. Fortunately, we didn't have to wait very long for a booth. The hot jasmine tea arrived quickly, and we ordered from a paper menu by checking off a number of photos of their dim sum items. These dishes were delivered straight from the kitchen. There were no carts in sight to mediate the traditional yum cha experience.

        The Shrimp Dumplings ($3.85) came with a decent amount of shrimp with an exterior that could be more refined. It was decent, but not outstanding.  

        The Bean Curd Skin with Pork and Shrimp ($3.85) came with slippery dried bean curd skin. The filling was a bit off balanced with more pork than shrimp.

        The Curry Baby Cuttlefish ($4.25) were very tender and nicely steeped in a yellow Chinese curry. I wished the curry flavor could be more pointed, but it was our favorite item due to its creativity and taste.

        The Pan-Fried Turnip Cake ($3.85) was made with grated turnip with touches of Chinese BBQ pork here and there. The turnip was tender, but I wished it came with crispy edges to add dimensions to this dish. It was decent, but not outstanding.

        The Crab and Chicken Congee  ($8.95) came with a large bowl of boiling crab and chicken congee. There were plenty of crab and chicken, but the crab was not de-shelled, and made it very difficult to consume. The consistency of the congee was decent, but not as thick and creamy as I would have liked. Not recommended.

        Service was prompt and efficient. The experience reminded me of Jing Fong, or many of the fast food dim sum spots on the streets of San Francisco. Despite the strengths and shortcomings described, I believe this can be a good place for groups, for people who don't have regular access to authentic dim sum restaurants, and for late night dining. I hope to find better dim sum restaurants in the area before coming back.
